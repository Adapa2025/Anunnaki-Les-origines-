The question “Who created the Anunnaki?” leads to a deep exploration of ancient Mesopotamian cosmology and the origins of religious belief. This video traces how the Anunnaki—figures like Anu, Enlil, and Enki—were not creators in the absolute sense but divine administrators born from earlier cosmic forces. In ancient Sumer, the eight-pointed star and the Dingir cuneiform symbol marked the divine, signifying that gods came from the sky. These symbols weren’t decorative—they represented real celestial beings.  
  
The term “Elohim,” often translated as “God,” was originally plural. Ancient texts show that early Hebrews believed in a divine council, not a singular deity. Yahweh himself was once one of many Canaanite gods and evolved over time to become the central figure in monotheistic religion. Archaeology and scripture reveal that Yahweh absorbed traits from older deities like El and Baal and may have been linked to the goddess Asherah. This transformation from polytheism to monotheism happened through social, political, and theological engineering, not revelation.  
  
Gnostic texts provide another perspective by introducing the Demiurge—a false creator who proclaims himself the only god. Gnostics identified this being with Yahweh, suggesting that what many worship as the ultimate god is actually a limited, flawed entity. In contrast, Gnosticism, Hermeticism, Hinduism, and even Spinozan philosophy point to a higher source—formless, eternal, and beyond any singular god figure. This source, known as Brahman, the All, or Deus sive Natura, is not a being but the essence of existence itself.  
  
Cosmogonies from Sumer, Babylon, Egypt, Greece, and India consistently show that creation was not the work of one omnipotent god, but a generational process where gods emerged from chaos and water. Enuma Elish, the Babylonian creation epic, describes how Marduk formed the world from Tiamat’s body. In Egyptian mythology, the universe emerges from the formless waters of Nun. The Greeks begin with Chaos, while Hinduism describes an eternal cycle powered by Brahman. Even Genesis borrows from these myths, shaping ancient motifs into a monotheistic narrative.  
  
Philosophers like Spinoza later rejected the idea of a distant, judgmental god and instead saw God as identical with nature itself. His idea that everything is a reflection of divine substance aligns with older spiritual views that emphasized direct experience of the divine. The video explores how these traditions lead to a more expansive, non-dogmatic understanding of divinity—one that does not depend on fear, obedience, or exclusivity.  
  
Religious institutions historically used theology to justify political power. Kings claimed divine mandate, and priestly elites centralized worship to control societies. Over time, this led to a rigid model of salvation and damnation that contradicted the original pluralistic, inclusive spiritual traditions. Organized religion has offered moral guidance but also caused division and suppression of spiritual freedom.  
  
The video concludes by proposing that the true source of all creation lies beyond all known gods, scriptures, and traditions. It is formless, timeless, and present in all things. This source does not require worship, names, or monuments—it only asks for remembrance. The Anunnaki, Yahweh, and even the Demiurge are seen as intermediaries, not ultimate origins. The real journey is toward the silent source that was never lost—only forgotten.

**35:51** · It doesn’t matter the religion. If it stirs division, if it says we are saved and they are not... Look... from my point of view, that is not religion! But I do believe.

**36:08** · I believe in the message of the enlightened ones who have walked among us: Buddha, Jesus, and the other prophets who came not to found dogmas, but to open hearts. I believe more in their messages than in the stories people tell about them. I believe more in the message they left than in the religions that were founded through them. Because, from my point of view, organized religions have great and positive value, but also a negative and problematic counterpart.

**36:40** · This is the God above all gods. Not in hierarchy, but in truth.

**36:44** · Not a king on a throne, but the silence beneath all sound, the light behind all light.

**36:51** · He does not demand monuments. He does not require names. He does not command worship—because He is the world, and we are part of Him. All creation pulses with His being.

**37:01** · In the silence behind mythologies, behind pantheons, behind temples, exists the ineffable Source. Beyond Anu. Beyond the Anunnaki. Beyond every sky father and demiurge.

**37:16** · Lies the Nameless, Eternal Reality—formless, omnipresent, silent. And now, standing at the threshold of that silence, the journey continues—not toward creation, not toward a next deity, but into the mystery that preceded all words and gods.

### The God

**37:45** · At the edge of our journey, where words dissolve and myth meets mystery, we encounter the silent starting point—the primordial waters. Thales of Miletus, the first philosopher of our Western tradition, spoke a profound truth: “All is water.” Not in the literal sense, but as the stream of origin—the arche, the boundless essence from which earth, sky, soul, and time emerge. From this living ocean springs not just physical life, but cosmic potential—the silent field upon which story and form are etched. This is the divine womb, the invisible sea from which existence is born.

**38:30** · And the ancients knew it. The Enuma Elish begins with Tiamat and Apsu—salt and fresh waters—from whose vast bodies the gods themselves were born. In Egyptian myth, Nun is the infinite water, the divine nothingness from which Atum arises. These waters are more than poetic symbols; they are sacred depths—the eternal mother and father of form and formlessness.

**38:54** · From these waters emerge archetypes—not gods in robes, but living cosmic truths.

**39:00** · The Primordial Mother, nurturing and generative; the Primordial Father, ordering and initiating; both embedded within the Source itself. Not separate, but distinct expressions within the One. They are the breath and the fire, the pulse and the pattern. They do not stand beside the Source—they are the Source in movement. And from them, something wondrous happens.

**39:26** · The Son is born—not as a historical person, but as a principle of emergence. The Logos. The Word.

**39:34** · The Flame in motion. This is not a hierarchy, but a mystery—the eternal One becoming Three.

**39:41** · The Mother births space, the Father shapes it, and the Son fills it with life. Creation isn’t a one-time event—it is the eternal emanation of the divine reality expressing itself through trinity.

**39:59** · In Hermetic writings and in certain Gnostic teachings, a cognate triad appears. Hermetic texts portray the Father (as the Mind) emanating through the Logos—the “Son”—into Nature or Matter as the receptive matrix; Peratic Gnostics name the triad Patēr (or Father), Huios (or Son), and Hulē (or Matter). In this vision, the Father emits the forms, the Son conveys them, and Matter receives them as the living canvas.

**40:32** · Creation is not a command, but a transmission of presence—a dance of cosmic love. The Astral Light—the radiant current behind matter—is the breath of the One becoming visible. This mystery lives in the quiet center of consciousness. It whispers not in temples or books, but in silence—in the space between breaths. It lives in you too. It is known in moments of clarity, in awe under the stars, in the hush after grief, in the laughter of a child.

**41:08** · The One is not a figure to be feared, but the ground of all being—the Mother and Father and Child inside your breath.

**41:27** · “The true God never spoke. He simply is. He did not create the world to be worshiped — but to be experienced. And when we search outward, we forget… we are part of Him.”

**41:41** · We descend now into that silence. Not to escape—but to remember. Because in the stillness, the questions return, not as doubt but as doorways: Who is the god you’ve been worshiping?

**41:58** · Is he truly the Source… or a shadow of power, shaped by fear and myth? Is he a creator… or an impostor? A jealous ruler demanding obedience—or a demiurge masking as light? And most importantly: What if the true God… never had a name? What if the true God didn’t demand rituals or monuments, obedience or belief? What if the true God only asks this: that you remember?

**42:29** · Remember you were never separate. Remember the primordial waters flow within your blood.

**42:35** · Remember that beyond Anu, beyond Yahweh, beyond all thrones and heavens… there is only Being. The Nameless Source. Formless. Timeless. Intimate. And perhaps the end of all our seeking is not a revelation—but a return. A return to what was never lost. A return to the One. The One in Three.

**43:01** · The Three within One. Not a creed—but a communion. Not worship—but awareness. Not fear—but freedom.

**43:13** · And in the hush that follows the final word, we find what we always sought: The silence before the first sound. The stillness before the first motion.

**43:28** · The God who is, was, and will always be— without name, without face, without need.

**43:36** · Only love. Only being. Only now. This is the end of the journey. And it is only the beginning.
