The Anunnaki : The Resurrection of Inanna dramatizes the myth of Inanna's Descent to the Netherworld (Underworld), bringing this ancient story to life. The Myth tells the story of Inanna, also known as the morning star, Venus, Ishtar, the star, and, later, Lucifer—a term derived from Greek meaning "light-bringer" and associated with Venus by the Romans, who adopted this title from Babylonian mythology, where Inanna was revered as Ishtar. This myth is one of the earliest accounts of resurrection in recorded mythology, marking Inanna's descent, death, and return to life as a foundational resurrection story. Inanna, embodying the role of the morning star, chooses to abandon her place in the heavens to venture into the underworld, the dark, enigmatic realm ruled by her sister Ereshkigal.  
  
As she prepares for her descent, Inanna gathers her seven divine powers, including a crown, beads of lapis lazuli, and a pectoral inscribed with "Come, man, come," each symbolizing her authority and celestial status. As she passes through each of the seven gates of the underworld, she surrenders these symbols one by one, gradually giving up her divine identity and power. When she arrives in Ereshkigal's domain, Inanna stands defenseless before the seven judges of the underworld, the Anunnaki, who pronounce her death sentence, turning her lifeless body into a corpse hung upon a hook—a stark symbol of sacrifice and transformation.  
  
For three days, Inanna’s servant Ninshubur mourns her above, pleading with the gods to revive her. She seeks the help of Enlil and Nanna, who refuse aid, saying Inanna has overstepped her bounds by seeking the powers of the underworld. Finally, Ninshubur turns to Enki, the god of wisdom, who, moved by her devotion, creates two beings, the kur-jara and the gala-tura, to enter the underworld carrying life-giving water and plants. Through compassion, they comfort the grieving Ereshkigal and persuade her to release Inanna's body. They anoint her with the life-giving elements, and Inanna is resurrected.  
  
The Anunnaki decree that no one may leave the underworld without providing a substitute, and demons escort Inanna back to the surface, demanding she offer someone in her place. Inanna spares her faithful followers but ultimately condemns her husband, Dumuzi, who becomes the substitute.
